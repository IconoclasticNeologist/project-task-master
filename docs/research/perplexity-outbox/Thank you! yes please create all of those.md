Here’s a set of **app‑ready modules** built from the gap‑fill research, with **short, trauma‑informed cards** and clear “Ask your advocate/lawyer” prompts. ## Module 1 – Plea and Your Voice

### Card 1 – Most cases end in pleas

Body:
In the U.S., most criminal cases end in plea agreements, not trials. This means many survivors never testify in front of a jury. Instead, the case may be resolved by a guilty plea and move to sentencing. This is about how the system works, not about your worth or the importance of your story.[^1][^2][^3]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate how pleas usually work in your court and what that could mean for you.[^4]
Tag: general federal information

### Card 2 – Your right to be heard on pleas

Body:
Federal law says crime victims have the right to be “reasonably heard” at public hearings about release, plea, and sentencing. This can include speaking or submitting a written statement about how the crime affected you. The judge and prosecutor still decide whether to accept a plea.[^5][^6][^7]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or a victims’‑rights lawyer how you can share your views on any proposed plea.[^8][^9]
Tag: general federal information / jurisdiction‑dependent

### Card 3 – Feelings when a case settles early

Body:
If your case ends with a plea, you might feel relief, anger, or disappointment. You may have prepared yourself to testify and then never take the stand. These mixed feelings are common. Support services and advocates can help you process them.[^10][^3][^11][^12]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate about emotional support if your case ends in a plea instead of a trial.[^12]
Tag: support/wellbeing

## Module 2 – Your Own Lawyer (Victims’‑Rights Attorneys)

### Card 4 – The prosecutor is not your lawyer

Body:
The government’s lawyer (the prosecutor) represents the United States, not you as an individual. Federal law gives you the right to “confer” with that lawyer, but that does not create an attorney‑client relationship. The prosecutor must follow the law and office policy, even when victims disagree.[^13][^5]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate to explain what the “right to confer” means in your case.[^6][^13]
Tag: general federal information

### Card 5 – Getting your own victims’‑rights attorney

Body:
In some places, victims can get their own lawyer to help enforce rights like notice, presence, privacy, and being heard. Groups such as the National Crime Victim Law Institute (NCVLI) run “Rights Enforcement Clinics” that provide free legal help in certain cases. Availability depends on where you live.[^14][^9][^15][^16]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or victim‑witness staff if there are victims’‑rights clinics or legal‑aid programs in your area.[^15]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent

### Card 6 – Why your own lawyer can help

Body:
A victims’‑rights lawyer can speak for you in court on issues like delays, privacy, and impact statements. They help make sure rights that exist “on paper” actually get used. This lawyer does not replace the prosecutor but focuses on your rights and safety.[^17][^18][^14]
Ask your advocate:
Ask a victims’‑rights clinic or legal‑aid office what kinds of help they can and cannot give.[^9]
Tag: general information / support

## Module 3 – Trafficking‑Specific Question Patterns

### Card 7 – “Why didn’t you leave?”

Body:
Trafficking survivors are often asked, “Why didn’t you leave?” or “Why did you go back?” during questioning. Research shows many survivors stay or return because of fear, threats, money control, immigration worries, and emotional bonds with the trafficker. These behaviors are common in trafficking and do not prove consent.[^19][^20][^21][^22]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate to explain, in general, why courts are warned about myths like “real victims just run.”[^23][^19]
Tag: support/wellbeing

### Card 8 – “You called him your boyfriend”

Body:
Some survivors describe a trafficker as a “boyfriend” or romantic partner. Defense lawyers may point to this to suggest the relationship was loving or consensual. Studies show traffickers often use gifts, praise, and affection mixed with abuse to create trauma bonds. This can make feelings and words very complicated.[^21][^22][^24][^25]
Ask your advocate:
Ask a trauma‑trained advocate to explain, in general terms, what a “trauma bond” can look like.[^24][^21]
Tag: support/wellbeing

### Card 9 – Delayed and changing stories

Body:
Many trafficking survivors tell their story slowly, in pieces, sometimes changing details over time. This can happen because of fear, shame, confusion, trauma, or language issues. International guidance warns courts not to treat every inconsistency as proof someone is lying.[^26][^27][^28][^23]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate to explain how trauma and control can affect when and how survivors speak.[^28][^29]
Tag: support/wellbeing

*(Your app should remind users that these patterns are about expectations and systems, not instructions on how to answer questions.)*

## Module 4 – Changes in Your Story and Memory

### Card 10 – What “impeachment” means

Body:
In court, “impeachment” means trying to challenge a witness’s credibility. Lawyers may point to earlier statements that differ from what you say on the stand. The rules say you are usually given a chance to explain or deny those differences.[^30][^31]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or victims’‑rights lawyer how impeachment and prior statements are handled in your court.[^13]
Tag: general federal information

### Card 11 – Refreshing your memory

Body:
Sometimes a witness looks at a note, report, or other writing to “refresh” their memory. After that, the witness is supposed to answer from present memory, not read the document aloud as evidence. The other side can see and ask questions about what you used to refresh your memory.[^32][^33]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate how your court usually handles “refreshing recollection” for witnesses.[^34]
Tag: general federal information

### Card 12 – Trauma and inconsistent memories

Body:
Brain research shows that traumatic memories can be strong and clear in some parts but scattered or partly missing in others. Survivors might change the order of events or remember new details later. Experts say inconsistency alone does not prove dishonesty.[^27][^35][^28]
Ask your advocate:
Ask a trauma‑informed professional to explain how trauma can affect memory over time.[^36][^37]
Tag: support/wellbeing

## Module 5 – Internet, Social Media, and Contact Rules

### Card 13 – Talking with other witnesses

Body:
Courts often order witnesses not to discuss their testimony with each other until the case is finished. This helps keep each person’s story their own. Breaking this rule can lead to questions about credibility or, in some cases, limits on using your testimony.[^38][^39][^40]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate what your judge’s rules are about talking with other witnesses.[^39]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent

### Card 14 – Reading or posting about the case

Body:
Guidance for police and prosecutors warns that social‑media posts and reading online coverage can affect cases. Seeing other versions of events may change how a witness remembers things. Public posts about the case or jurors can also cause problems.[^41][^42]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate what your court says about social media, news, and online comments during the case.[^41]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent

### Card 15 – Contact with jurors or the accused

Body:
Witnesses are usually told not to talk to jurors or privately approach the person accused about the case. Any contact should be through official channels, like the prosecutor or victim‑witness staff. This protects the fairness and safety of everyone.[^43][^44]
Ask your advocate:
Ask victim‑witness staff to explain safe ways to raise concerns without direct contact with jurors or the accused.[^45][^46]
Tag: general information

## Module 6 – Immigration and Safety (Foreign‑National Survivors)

### Card 16 – T visas and U visas (overview)

Body:
Some foreign‑national survivors may qualify for special immigration protections called T or U visas. These require meeting strict legal rules about being a victim of certain crimes and, in many cases, helping law enforcement. Getting one is not automatic just because you testify.[^47][^48][^49][^50]
Ask your advocate:
Ask an immigration attorney or legal‑aid group whether a T or U visa might apply in your situation.[^51][^52]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent (US immigration)

### Card 17 – Continued Presence

Body:
In trafficking cases, law enforcement can sometimes request “Continued Presence,” a temporary status that lets identified trafficking victims stay in the U.S. and get work permission while the case is investigated. This status is short‑term and depends on law enforcement action, not just on testifying.[^53][^54]
Ask your advocate:
Ask an immigration lawyer or advocate what Continued Presence is and whether it is an option for you.[^54][^51]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent (US immigration)

### Card 18 – Confidentiality protections

Body:
Federal law (8 U.S.C. § 1367) limits how government agencies can share information from some immigration cases, including certain VAWA, T, and U applications. These rules aim to stop abusers and traffickers from using immigration systems against victims. They do not cover every situation.[^55][^56]
Ask your advocate:
Ask an immigration attorney to explain what confidentiality protections do—and do not—cover in your case.[^55]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent

*(This module should carry a clear disclaimer: immigration decisions are complex and must be discussed with a qualified immigration lawyer.)*

## Module 7 – Special Courtroom Supports for Adults

### Card 19 – Screens and video testimony (international examples)

Body:
Some countries allow adult vulnerable witnesses to testify behind a screen or by video link so they do not have to face the accused directly. Canada, the UK, and parts of Australia use these aids in certain sexual‑offence and trafficking cases when needed for a “full and candid account.”[^57][^58][^59][^60][^61]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or prosecutor what, if any, special measures are allowed for adult witnesses in your court.[^44][^62]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent / international

### Card 20 – Pre‑recorded cross‑examination (UK)

Body:
In England and Wales, some vulnerable victims and witnesses can have their evidence and cross‑examination recorded before trial under section 28 of the Youth Justice and Criminal Evidence Act. The video is then played for the jury later. This reduces repeated court appearances.[^60][^63][^64]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate how other countries use pre‑recorded testimony and whether anything similar exists where you live.[^65]
Tag: international information

### Card 21 – Facility dogs and support animals

Body:
In some U.S. courts, specially trained “facility dogs” are allowed to sit with witnesses—including adults—to provide quiet comfort while they testify. The judge decides whether this is allowed and how it works so it does not distract or unfairly influence the jury.[^66][^67]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate whether your courthouse has a facility‑dog program and how witnesses can request it.[^67][^66]
Tag: jurisdiction‑dependent

## Module 8 – Waiting, Delay, and Anxiety

### Card 22 – Your right to avoid unfair delay

Body:
Federal law says crime victims have the right to proceedings “free from unreasonable delay.” But some delays still happen for reasons like scheduling, new evidence, or legal motions. This can be frustrating and stressful for survivors.[^3][^68][^5]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or victims’‑rights lawyer what counts as “unreasonable” delay and how to raise concerns.[^13]
Tag: general federal information

### Card 23 – Why delays happen

Body:
Cases may be postponed because witnesses are unavailable, lawyers need more time, or plea talks are underway. These reasons are about the system, not about you personally. Knowing why a hearing moved can sometimes ease uncertainty.[^11][^1][^3]
Ask your advocate:
Ask victim‑witness staff to explain, in simple terms, why any delay happened and what to expect next.[^11][^45]
Tag: support/wellbeing

### Card 24 – Coping with waiting

Body:
Research shows that long waits in court cases can increase anxiety and low mood for many victims. Trauma‑informed care suggests using grounding skills, regular check‑ins with supporters, and predictable routines to help manage the stress of “not knowing.”[^69][^70][^10][^11]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or counselor to plan coping steps for days when court might be delayed again.[^12][^69]
Tag: support/wellbeing

***

## Module 9 – Quick Grounding Tools for Court Days

### Card 25 – Slow breathing

Body:
Studies show that slow, guided breathing can reduce anxiety for many people, at least in the short term. You can try breathing in gently through your nose, then slowly out through your mouth, focusing on long, steady breaths. This is a simple tool, not a cure for trauma.[^71][^72][^73]
Ask your advocate:
Ask a counselor or health professional to show you a safe slow‑breathing exercise you can use on court days.[^72]
Tag: support/wellbeing

### Card 26 – 5‑4‑3‑2‑1 grounding

Body:
Clinicians often use the “5‑4‑3‑2‑1” technique: notice 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, and 1 you taste. This can help bring attention back to the present when memories or feelings feel overwhelming.[^70][^74][^69]
Ask your advocate:
Ask a trauma‑trained professional to walk you through grounding exercises like 5‑4‑3‑2‑1.[^70]
Tag: support/wellbeing

### Card 27 – Using simple sensory tools

Body:
Guides suggest simple sensory tools, like holding something textured, feeling your feet firmly on the floor, or briefly using cool water on your hands, to help your body settle. These tools are optional; you can choose what feels helpful and safe for you.[^75][^70]
Ask your advocate:
Ask your advocate or therapist which grounding tools fit your needs and any health limits you may have.[^69][^75]
Tag: support/wellbeing
