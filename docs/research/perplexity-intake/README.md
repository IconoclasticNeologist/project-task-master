## 1. SCOPE AND VARIATION

### Key types of proceedings

- **Hearing (pre‑trial or motions)**  
  A hearing is a court session where the judge decides specific issues, such as bail, motions about evidence, or scheduling. Survivors may attend as witnesses or observers, but not every hearing includes testimony. What happens at hearings varies by case type, judge, and jurisdiction. [judiciallearningcenter](https://judiciallearningcenter.org/student-center/how-the-courts-work/your-day-in-court/)

- **Deposition (rare in criminal cases)**  
  In federal criminal cases, a deposition is a formal out‑of‑court questioning of a witness to preserve testimony in “exceptional circumstances,” under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 15. The witness answers questions under oath, and the transcript can be used later at trial if allowed by the rules of evidence. Depositions are ordered by the court and are uncommon in federal criminal human‑trafficking cases. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule15/)

- **Grand‑jury appearance (federal)**  
  A federal grand jury is a group of 16–23 citizens who hear evidence from prosecutors to decide whether there is probable cause to charge someone with a federal crime. Proceedings are held in private, and the grand jury does not decide guilt or innocence. Witnesses may be subpoenaed to testify, and rules about lawyers in the room and what can be disclosed are set by federal law and practice. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule17/)

- **Trial testimony**  
  At a criminal trial, witnesses testify in open court before a judge and often a jury. Survivor‑witnesses may be called by the government or, less commonly, by the defense, and they answer questions on direct examination and cross‑examination under the rules of evidence and procedure. Trial rules differ between federal and state courts and can vary by judge. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre)

- **Sentencing**  
  If there is a conviction, the court holds a sentencing hearing where the judge decides the sentence. Under the Crime Victims’ Rights Act and Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32, crime victims have the right to be “reasonably heard” at a public sentencing proceeding, which can include a written or spoken impact statement. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2011-title18/pdf/USCODE-2011-title18-partII-chap237-sec3771.pdf)

- **Post‑conviction proceedings**  
  After sentencing, there may be appeals, parole hearings, or motions that can affect a defendant’s status and sometimes involve victim notification or participation. Victim‑notification rights at parole and post‑conviction stages are defined by federal law and by each state’s statutes and policies. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2023_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)

### What varies and what is more consistent

- **Varies by jurisdiction (federal vs. state/county)**  
  - Whether depositions of witnesses are used in criminal cases. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule15/)
  - Specific victims’ rights in state court and how they are enforced. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/sites/g/files/xyckuh226/files/publications/infores/rural_victim_assistance/page2.html)
  - Procedures and standards for remote testimony, closed courtrooms, and protective measures. [ncpro.sog.unc](https://ncpro.sog.unc.edu/manual/703-8)

- **Varies by charge and case type**  
  - Use of grand juries (required for many federal felonies, may differ in state practice). [bbc.co](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-us-canada-40823390)
  - Application of sexual‑history “rape shield” rules like Federal Rule of Evidence 412 and state equivalents. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre/rule_412)
  - Availability of special accommodations (for example, in cases involving sexual offenses or vulnerable witnesses). [www2.gov.bc](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/assets/gov/law-crime-and-justice/criminal-justice/bc-criminal-justice-system/if-victim/publications/court-lets-go-child-youth-witness.pdf)

- **Varies by age and role (witness vs. party)**  
  - Children and youth often have additional protections, such as modified courtroom settings and increased use of closed‑circuit television, which may not apply to adults. [aacap](https://www.aacap.org/aacap/Policy_Statements/1986/Protecting_Children_Undergoing_Abuse_Investigations_and_Testimony.aspx)
  - A survivor who is also a defendant or civil party has different rights and risks than a survivor who is solely a witness. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/witness)
  - Disability‑related accommodations may apply to any party, witness, juror, or spectator, but procedures for requesting them differ among courts. [probonodeskmanual.loyno](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/book/export/html/621)

## 2. COURT‑HEARING JOURNEY

For each stage below, examples are based mainly on U.S. federal criminal practice, with notes where state/county practice may differ. Experiences still vary by case, judge, and jurisdiction. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/enrd/environmental-crime-victim-assistance/rights-victims)

### A. Before court

- **What may happen**  
  - You may receive a subpoena ordering you to appear and testify. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule17/)
  - A victim‑witness coordinator or advocate may contact you about logistics, rights, and services. [popcenter.asu](https://popcenter.asu.edu/sites/default/files/problems/witness_intimidation/PDFs/USDOJ_OAG_2000.pdf)
  - You may be told about safety planning, separate waiting areas, and transportation options. [navaa](https://navaa.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NewDirectionsfromtheField.pdf)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can ask the prosecutor’s office or victim‑witness unit for information about the process, your rights, and possible accommodations. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/enrd/environmental-crime-victim-assistance/rights-victims)
  - You may choose whether to accept some services, such as counseling or accompaniment, even if they are offered. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/funding/performance-measures/iaa-ag-guidelines/ag-pmt-questionnaire.pdf)
  - You can ask about emotional support persons, interpreters, and disability accommodations. [najit](https://najit.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/09/benchcardohio.pdf)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Explaining basic court steps without telling you what to say in testimony. [ovcttac](https://www.ovcttac.gov/TaskForceGuide/EGuide/)
  - Helping with practical planning: transport, childcare, food, and time off work. [youthlaw](https://youthlaw.org/resources/how-to-support-youth-impacted-by-commercial-sexual-exploitation-during-testimony/)
  - Supporting safety planning, such as separate entrances or security escorts if available. [innovatingjustice](https://www.innovatingjustice.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/CCI_FactSheet_DV_Support_Checklist_03232021.pdf)

- **Plain‑language explanation (50–90 words)**  
  Before court, someone from the prosecutor’s office or a victim‑services program may contact you to talk about the hearing and answer questions. They might help with travel, safety planning, and understanding your rights. You can ask for an interpreter or other disability help if you need it. These helpers can explain what usually happens in court but should not tell you what your story should be. [oig.justice](https://oig.justice.gov/reports/OJP/a0608/findings.htm)

### B. Arrival and security

- **What may happen**  
  - You may go through security screening similar to an airport, including metal detectors and bag checks. [courts.alaska](https://courts.alaska.gov/selfhelp/debt/courtroom.htm)
  - Security officers enforce rules about weapons and some items. [innovatingjustice](https://www.innovatingjustice.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/CCI_FactSheet_DV_Support_Checklist_03232021.pdf)
  - You may be directed to a waiting area or courtroom by signage or staff. [courts.alaska](https://courts.alaska.gov/selfhelp/debt/courtroom.htm)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can ask court staff or your advocate where to wait and whether there is a separate area away from the accused. [popcenter.asu](https://popcenter.asu.edu/sites/default/files/problems/witness_intimidation/PDFs/USDOJ_OAG_2000.pdf)
  - If you have mobility, sensory, or communication needs, you can ask for help accessing the building and courtroom. [courts.michigan](https://www.courts.michigan.gov/48cfa4/siteassets/publications/manuals/ada/ada-handbook.pdf)
  - In some courts, you can request to avoid direct contact with the accused in hallways or waiting rooms, though this is not always possible. [navaa](https://navaa.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NewDirectionsfromtheField.pdf)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Meeting you outside or inside the courthouse and walking with you through security. [rensco](https://www.rensco.com/203/Victim-Services)
  - Asking court or security staff about separate waiting areas and any safety protocols. [innovatingjustice](https://www.innovatingjustice.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/CCI_FactSheet_DV_Support_Checklist_03232021.pdf)
  - Helping you understand courtroom layout (where the judge, jury, lawyers, and witness stand are). [flnd.uscourts](https://www.flnd.uscourts.gov/role-judge-and-other-courtroom-participants)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  At the courthouse, you will likely pass through security, where officers may scan you and your bag to keep everyone safe. Then you will be directed to the right floor or courtroom. An advocate can meet you, walk with you, and help ask for a waiting area away from the person accused if the court can offer that. You can also ask staff for disability‑related help, such as elevators or clear directions. [probonodeskmanual.loyno](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/book/export/html/621)

### C. Waiting before testimony

- **What may happen**  
  - You may wait in a hallway, waiting room, or separate space until your case is called. [popcenter.asu](https://popcenter.asu.edu/sites/default/files/problems/witness_intimidation/PDFs/USDOJ_OAG_2000.pdf)
  - You might see other witnesses, court staff, or the accused at a distance. [courts.alaska](https://courts.alaska.gov/selfhelp/debt/courtroom.htm)
  - Changes in schedule can mean extra waiting time. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2020_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You may ask the victim‑witness coordinator or advocate about approximate timing, though exact times can change. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ndin/victim-witness)
  - In some courts, you can wait in a separate area from defense witnesses when feasible. [navaa](https://navaa.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NewDirectionsfromtheField.pdf)
  - You can use grounding or coping skills, take brief walks nearby, or ask for breaks in waiting when allowed. [lajudicialcollege](https://lajudicialcollege.org/ljc/cle/2024SS%20Trauma.pdf)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Checking in with court staff about timing and any delays. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/library/publications/attorney-general-guidelines-victim-and-witness-assistance-2022-edition)
  - Helping you use calm, plain strategies to manage anxiety while waiting. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454/pdf/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454.pdf)
  - Making sure you know where bathrooms, water, and exits are. [courts.alaska](https://courts.alaska.gov/selfhelp/debt/courtroom.htm)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  You may spend time waiting before you talk in court. This can feel long, and plans can change during the day. An advocate or victim‑witness staff member can ask about timing, show you where to sit, and help with simple coping skills, like breathing or taking short breaks nearby if allowed. Some courts try to give survivors separate waiting areas, but this depends on space and local practice. [lajudicialcollege](https://lajudicialcollege.org/ljc/cle/2024SS%20Trauma.pdf)

### D. Direct examination

- **What may happen**  
  - The lawyer who called you (usually the prosecutor) asks you questions first; this is direct examination. [judiciallearningcenter](https://judiciallearningcenter.org/student-center/how-the-courts-work/your-day-in-court/)
  - You are sworn or affirmed to tell the truth, then sit in the witness stand. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/court_witness)
  - Questions aim to bring out your own experiences and observations relevant to the case. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You may look at the judge or a neutral point if looking at the accused feels hard; courts sometimes allow this. [www2.gov.bc](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/assets/gov/law-crime-and-justice/criminal-justice/bc-criminal-justice-system/if-victim/publications/court-lets-go-child-youth-witness.pdf)
  - You can say “I don’t know” or “I don’t remember” rather than guessing. [domesticpeace](https://domesticpeace.ca/images/uploads/documents/MicrosoftWord-InformationandTipsforTestifyinginCriminalCourtAugust2009.pdf)
  - If you do not understand a question, you can ask for it to be repeated or clarified. [custodyxchange](https://www.custodyxchange.com/topics/custody/steps/testify-in-family-court.php)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Explaining, ahead of time, the roles of judge, lawyers, jury, and witness, without shaping your testimony. [ovcttac](https://www.ovcttac.gov/TaskForceGuide/EGuide/)
  - Reminding you of basic rights like asking for clarification or a break through the court. [aacap](https://www.aacap.org/aacap/Policy_Statements/1986/Protecting_Children_Undergoing_Abuse_Investigations_and_Testimony.aspx)
  - Supporting you emotionally before and after you testify, not during the active questioning. [youthlaw](https://youthlaw.org/resources/how-to-support-youth-impacted-by-commercial-sexual-exploitation-during-testimony/)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  When it is your turn, you will go to the witness stand and promise to tell the truth. The prosecutor usually asks you questions first, to help the court understand what you saw, heard, or experienced. You can say you do not know or cannot remember instead of guessing, and you can ask the judge or lawyer to repeat or explain a question if it is confusing. Looking at the judge or a neutral spot is often allowed. [custodyxchange](https://www.custodyxchange.com/topics/custody/steps/testify-in-family-court.php)

### E. Cross‑examination

- **What may happen**  
  - After direct examination, the defense lawyer may question you; this is cross‑examination. [miwd.uscourts](https://www.miwd.uscourts.gov/sites/miwd/files/RHB_Courtroom_Protocol.pdf)
  - Questions may be short, leading, or framed for “yes” or “no” answers as allowed by the rules. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre)
  - The judge can limit improper or irrelevant questions under evidence rules, including sexual‑history protections. [ncpro.sog.unc](https://ncpro.sog.unc.edu/manual/703-8)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can answer only the question asked and avoid volunteering extra information unless the judge directs. [miwd.uscourts](https://www.miwd.uscourts.gov/sites/miwd/files/RHB_Courtroom_Protocol.pdf)
  - You can say “I don’t understand” and ask for a question to be repeated or rephrased. [custodyxchange](https://www.custodyxchange.com/topics/custody/steps/testify-in-family-court.php)
  - If you feel overwhelmed, you can ask the judge for a break through your own words or through the prosecutor. [www2.gov.bc](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/assets/gov/law-crime-and-justice/criminal-justice/bc-criminal-justice-system/if-victim/publications/court-lets-go-child-youth-witness.pdf)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Before court, explaining what cross‑examination is and why questions may feel challenging, without rehearsing your answers. [ovcttac](https://www.ovcttac.gov/TaskForceGuide/EGuide/)
  - Encouraging use of simple coping tools, like breathing and focusing on one question at a time. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454/pdf/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454.pdf)
  - Afterward, helping you process feelings and connect to further support. [oig.justice](https://oig.justice.gov/reports/OJP/a0608/findings.htm)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  After the first lawyer finishes, the other side’s lawyer may ask you questions. These may be short or push for “yes” or “no” answers and can feel stressful. You can answer only what is asked and ask the judge or lawyer to repeat or explain confusing questions. If you are too upset or need a bathroom break, you can ask the judge for a pause. [aacap](https://www.aacap.org/aacap/Policy_Statements/1986/Protecting_Children_Undergoing_Abuse_Investigations_and_Testimony.aspx)

### F. Breaks during testimony

- **What may happen**  
  - The judge may schedule breaks or call them as needed. [www2.gov.bc](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/assets/gov/law-crime-and-justice/criminal-justice/bc-criminal-justice-system/if-victim/publications/court-lets-go-child-youth-witness.pdf)
  - During breaks, you may be told not to discuss your testimony with other witnesses. [www2.gov.bc](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/assets/gov/law-crime-and-justice/criminal-justice/bc-criminal-justice-system/if-victim/publications/court-lets-go-child-youth-witness.pdf)
  - You may leave the witness stand but usually remain available nearby. [courts.alaska](https://courts.alaska.gov/selfhelp/debt/courtroom.htm)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can ask for a break if you feel dizzy, confused, or overwhelmed. [aacap](https://www.aacap.org/aacap/Policy_Statements/1986/Protecting_Children_Undergoing_Abuse_Investigations_and_Testimony.aspx)
  - You can use grounding skills, get water, or use the restroom during breaks. [lajudicialcollege](https://lajudicialcollege.org/ljc/cle/2024SS%20Trauma.pdf)
  - You may request to avoid contact with the accused during breaks when possible. [innovatingjustice](https://www.innovatingjustice.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/CCI_FactSheet_DV_Support_Checklist_03232021.pdf)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Being present in approved areas during breaks to offer emotional support. [ontario](https://www.ontario.ca/page/victimwitness-assistance-program)
  - Reminding you of any court instructions about not discussing testimony. [domesticpeace](https://domesticpeace.ca/images/uploads/documents/MicrosoftWord-InformationandTipsforTestifyinginCriminalCourtAugust2009.pdf)
  - Helping you use simple coping tools and plan what you need after leaving court. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454/pdf/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454.pdf)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  The judge can allow breaks during your time on the stand. If you feel sick, confused, or too upset, you can say so and ask for a pause. During breaks, you may be told not to talk about your answers with other witnesses until the case is finished. You can drink water, use the restroom, and use coping skills to calm your body. [lajudicialcollege](https://lajudicialcollege.org/ljc/cle/2024SS%20Trauma.pdf)

### G. After testimony (trial stage)

- **What may happen**  
  - After your testimony, you may be excused from the witness stand and possibly from further attendance, unless the court keeps you on “standby.” [flnd.uscourts](https://www.flnd.uscourts.gov/role-judge-and-other-courtroom-participants)
  - The case continues with other witnesses and evidence. [judiciallearningcenter](https://judiciallearningcenter.org/student-center/how-the-courts-work/your-day-in-court/)
  - The prosecutor or victim‑witness staff may check in with you. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ndin/victim-witness)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can ask whether you are free to leave or need to remain nearby. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ndin/victim-witness)
  - You can request information about next steps, such as verdict, sentencing dates, or notification processes. [fjc](https://www.fjc.gov/subject/crime-victims-rights)
  - You may choose to connect with counseling or support services, if available. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/program/human-trafficking/training-and-technical-assistance)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Explaining what happens next in the case and how you might be informed of outcomes. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/library/publications/attorney-general-guidelines-victim-and-witness-assistance-2022-edition)
  - Supporting you emotionally and helping with post‑testimony coping. [youthlaw](https://youthlaw.org/resources/how-to-support-youth-impacted-by-commercial-sexual-exploitation-during-testimony/)
  - Linking you to ongoing services or community supports. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/program/human-trafficking/training-and-technical-assistance)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  When you finish testifying, the judge will say whether you can leave or need to stay nearby. The trial keeps going with other witnesses and evidence. A victim‑witness worker or advocate may check on you and explain how you will learn about the verdict and, later, any sentencing. You can ask about counseling or other supports for after court. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/program/human-trafficking/training-and-technical-assistance)

### H. Sentencing

- **What may happen**  
  - If there is a conviction, the court sets a sentencing hearing. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2019_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)
  - Under 18 U.S.C. § 3771 and Fed. R. Crim. P. 32, victims have the right to be “reasonably heard” at sentencing, often through a victim impact statement. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule32/)
  - Restitution and other orders may be considered. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2023_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You may choose whether to submit a written or spoken victim impact statement, unless limited by law or court order. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2020_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)
  - You can ask the prosecutor or victim‑witness staff how sentencing works and whether you can attend. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/library/publications/attorney-general-guidelines-victim-and-witness-assistance-2022-edition)
  - You can request accommodations (interpreters, disability access) for any participation. [ada](https://www.ada.gov/resources/effective-communication/)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Explaining, in simple terms, what a victim impact statement is and what topics it may include, without writing it for you. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2023_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)
  - Supporting you in making choices about attending or submitting a statement. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/funding/performance-measures/iaa-ag-guidelines/ag-pmt-questionnaire.pdf)
  - Helping with practical needs for attending sentencing. [oig.justice](https://oig.justice.gov/reports/OJP/a0608/findings.htm)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  At sentencing, the judge decides the punishment. Federal law says crime victims have a right to be “reasonably heard” at this hearing, which can include a statement about how the crime affected them. You can ask the prosecutor or victim‑witness staff how this works and whether you want to attend or send a statement. You can also ask for interpreters or disability access if needed. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2011-title18/pdf/USCODE-2011-title18-partII-chap237-sec3771.pdf)

### I. After court (post‑sentencing)

- **What may happen**  
  - You may receive notifications about the defendant’s release, escape, or parole, depending on law and your preferences. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2011-title18/pdf/USCODE-2011-title18-partII-chap237-sec3771.pdf)
  - You may be informed of restitution processes or compensation programs that apply. [bu](https://www.bu.edu/pilj/files/2022/04/REVISED-7-Byrne-Carter-for-Submission.pdf)
  - You may engage in ongoing healing, safety planning, and possibly civil or immigration processes separate from the criminal case. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/sites/g/files/xyckuh226/files/model-standards/6/ovcttac-model-standards-508.pdf)

- **Choices or supports**  
  - You can choose whether to receive certain notifications and may change your mind over time, within program rules. [justice](https://www.justice.gov/enrd/environmental-crime-victim-assistance/rights-victims)
  - You can ask about restitution, unpaid wages, and compensation programs if they apply to your situation. [damorelaw](https://damorelaw.com/crime-victim-lawyer/)
  - You may seek ongoing trauma‑informed services, including health care and counseling. [who](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789241550147)

- **What an advocate can help with**  
  - Explaining post‑conviction notification options and helping complete necessary forms. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/funding/performance-measures/iaa-ag-guidelines/ag-pmt-questionnaire.pdf)
  - Supporting safety planning connected to release or parole information. [navaa](https://navaa.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NewDirectionsfromtheField.pdf)
  - Helping connect you to long‑term services for health, housing, and emotional support. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454/pdf/GOVPUB-J34-PURL-gpo155454.pdf)

- **Plain‑language explanation**  
  After sentencing, the case may feel “over,” but you could still get notices about the defendant’s status, like release or parole. You can choose whether to receive some of these notices in many systems. You may also be able to ask about restitution or compensation, including unpaid wages or medical costs, depending on the law. Advocates can help with forms and link you to longer‑term support and healing resources. [bu](https://www.bu.edu/pilj/files/2022/04/REVISED-7-Byrne-Carter-for-Submission.pdf)

## 3. RIGHTS, ACCOMMODATIONS, AND PROTECTIONS

This section highlights federal law and official guidance; state and county rules may add rights or limit how they work in local courts. [fjc](https://www.fjc.gov/subject/crime-victims-rights)

### A. Crime victims’ rights and notification

- **Federal rights (18 U.S.C. § 3771, CVRA)**  
  The Crime Victims’ Rights Act gives crime victims rights including: reasonable protection from the accused; reasonable, accurate, and timely notice of public court proceedings and release or escape; the right not to be excluded from public court proceedings (with limited exceptions); the right to be reasonably heard at release, plea, and sentencing; the right to confer with the government attorney; the right to full and timely restitution; proceedings free from unreasonable delay; and fairness and respect for dignity and privacy. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2020_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)

- **Services and notification (34 U.S.C. § 20141, VRRA; AG Guidelines)**  
  The Victims’ Rights and Restitution Act requires federal agencies to provide services such as information on medical and social services, protection, and case status, and to inform victims of their rights. Attorney General Guidelines direct Department of Justice staff to identify victims, notify them of rights, offer services, and give written descriptions of services. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/library/publications/attorney-general-guidelines-victim-and-witness-assistance-2011-edition-revised)

- **Jurisdiction notes**  
  Every state has its own victims’‑rights laws and, in many cases, constitutional amendments, which may define notice, presence, and participation differently than federal law. Survivors in state cases should ask local prosecutors’ offices or victim‑services agencies about state‑specific rights. [osig.virginia](https://www.osig.virginia.gov/media/governorvirginiagov/office-of-the-state-inspector-general/pdf/Victim-Witness-Brochure.pdf)

### B. Support person / advocate attendance

- **Federal guidance**  
  Federal victim‑services materials note that victims have the right to be accompanied to criminal proceedings by a family member, victim advocate, or other support person providing help, subject to court rules. Attorney General Guidelines emphasize a strong presumption in favor of providing assistance and services to victims, including accompaniment. [freedomnetworkusa](https://freedomnetworkusa.org/app/uploads/2018/11/DOJ-OVC-Legal-Rights-and-Needs-of-Victims-of-Human-Trafficking-in-the-United-States.pdf)

- **Jurisdiction variation**  
  Some courts may limit who can sit at counsel table or stay in the courtroom if a support person is also a witness. State laws may specifically permit or restrict support persons in sexual‑offense or trafficking cases. [ncpro.sog.unc](https://ncpro.sog.unc.edu/manual/703-8)

### C. Interpreters and disability accommodations

- **ADA and effective communication**  
  Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act requires state and local governments, including courts, to communicate effectively with people who have communication disabilities and to provide appropriate auxiliary aids and services when needed. DOJ guidance and ADA materials highlight qualified sign‑language interpreters, computer‑assisted transcription, and other aids for parties and witnesses. [sc-deaf](https://www.sc-deaf.org/uploads/1/1/9/4/119452553/stateandlocalcourts.pdf)

- **Requesting accommodations**  
  Many courts have ADA coordinators or procedures (often by form or oral request) to ask for accommodations, including interpreters, physical access changes, or remote participation. Federal guidance stresses that entities must give primary consideration to the person’s requested aid or service unless it would cause undue burden or fundamentally alter a program. [courts.ca](https://courts.ca.gov/sites/default/files/courts/default/2024-12/btb_23_4g_1.pdf)

- **Jurisdiction variation**  
  In criminal cases, courts generally bear responsibility for accommodations, while in civil matters individuals may need to arrange and pay for interpreters. Local rules and state law define specific processes and timelines for requests; survivors should ask clerks or ADA coordinators how to proceed. [courts.ca](https://courts.ca.gov/sites/default/files/courts/default/2024-12/btb_23_4g_1.pdf)

### D. Remote testimony and protective measures

- **Federal and state tools**  
  Courts sometimes use remote testimony (for example, closed‑circuit television) or modified courtroom arrangements, especially for children or vulnerable witnesses, to reduce trauma while respecting defendants’ rights. ADA and court‑access guidance note that remote participation may be a reasonable accommodation when physical barriers or disabilities limit in‑person attendance. [probonodeskmanual.loyno](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/court-access-individuals-disabilities/513-possible-accommodations)

- **Limits and variation**  
  Federal law and case‑law control when remote testimony is allowed in criminal trials, and rules differ among circuits; state courts follow their own statutes and constitutional standards. Survivors should ask prosecutors or victim‑witness staff whether any protective measures are legally available in their specific court. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre)

### E. Privacy, confidentiality, and court records

- **Federal protections**  
  The CVRA includes the right to be treated with respect for dignity and privacy. Rape‑shield rules such as Federal Rule of Evidence 412 generally prohibit evidence offered to prove that an alleged victim engaged in other sexual behavior or has a sexual predisposition, subject to complex exceptions and procedures. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-mied-2_17-cr-20183/pdf/USCOURTS-mied-2_17-cr-20183-34.pdf)

- **State rules and records**  
  State rape‑shield laws (such as North Carolina Rule 412) provide similar but not identical protections, often requiring in camera hearings before sexual‑history evidence is introduced. Court records are usually public, but some information may be sealed or limited by statute or court order; this varies widely by jurisdiction. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/sites/g/files/xyckuh226/files/publications/infores/rural_victim_assistance/page2.html)

### F. Restitution and compensation

- **Federal restitution and trafficking‑related rights**  
  The CVRA and related statutes protect the right to full and timely restitution. Trafficking survivors may seek restitution for unpaid wages and other losses connected to the crime. The Victims of Crime Act (VOCA) created funds to support compensation programs covering medical expenses, counseling, and certain other costs. [damorelaw](https://damorelaw.com/crime-victim-lawyer/)

- **Jurisdiction variation**  
  Each state operates its own compensation program with specific eligibility rules, covered crimes, and application processes. Survivors should ask local victim‑services offices or state compensation programs for details. [ontario](https://www.ontario.ca/page/victim-services-ontario)

### G. Safety planning and contact with the accused

- **Protections in and around court**  
  Victims have rights to reasonable protection from the accused under federal law. Best‑practice checklists for domestic‑violence and trauma‑informed court practices recommend separate waiting areas, clear bans on contact in the courthouse, and active monitoring by security. [govinfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2011-title18/pdf/USCODE-2011-title18-partII-chap237-sec3771.pdf)

- **Limits**  
  No system can guarantee complete safety; protection orders, safety plans, and court protocols reduce risk but cannot remove it. Survivors should discuss safety concerns with prosecutors, victim‑witness staff, and advocates to explore available measures. [oig.justice](https://oig.justice.gov/reports/OJP/a0608/findings.htm)

### H. Asking for an accommodation

- **How to ask**  
  Official guidance recommends that requests for accommodations describe the needed support and the impairment or condition that makes it necessary, and be made as early as possible, often at least several court days before the hearing. Requests may be written (using court forms) or oral, directed to ADA/access coordinators, clerks, or the judge, depending on local rules. [courts.michigan](https://www.courts.michigan.gov/48cfa4/siteassets/publications/manuals/ada/ada-handbook.pdf)

- **Trauma‑informed note**  
  Survivors can ask prosecutors, victim‑witness coordinators, or advocates to help them frame requests in plain language, focusing on what helps them participate (for example, breaks, seating changes, interpreters) without revealing more personal information than necessary. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/program/human-trafficking/training-and-technical-assistance)

## 4. TRAUMA‑INFORMED HEARING PREPARATION

This section offers general, evidence‑based ideas; it does not coach testimony or comment on credibility. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/jhrp/article-abstract/9/2/268/4031959)

### A. Grounding and coping before, during, and after court

- **Principles of trauma‑informed support**  
  Trauma‑informed approaches prioritize safety, choice, and control, and aim to avoid re‑traumatizing survivors. Guidance for supporting trafficking witnesses and other trauma survivors emphasizes predictable routines, clear explanations, and affirming dignity. [humanrights.stanford](https://humanrights.stanford.edu/sites/humanrights/files/media/file/2104429-trauma-informed_investigations_field_guide_web_0_0.pdf)

- **Example coping tools (non‑clinical)**  
  Resources recommend simple strategies such as slow breathing, focusing on present sensations, taking breaks, and having supportive people nearby. Psychoeducation—brief explanations of normal reactions like anxiety, numbness, or intrusive memories—can help survivors understand and manage their responses. [nctsn](https://www.nctsn.org/sites/default/files/resources/understanding_child_traumatic_stress_guide_for_parents.pdf)

### B. Memory gaps, changed order, and emotional responses (without credibility judgments)

- **What research says about trauma and memory**  
  Trauma can make memories intense and detailed for some people, but fragmented, out of order, or partly missing for others. Studies on trauma and memory show that people may have gaps, delayed recall, or changing narratives over time due to how the brain encodes and retrieves stressful events. [nij.ojp](https://nij.ojp.gov/library/publications/suppression-and-memory-childhood-traumatic-events-trauma-symptoms-and-non)

- **General implications (not case‑specific)**  
  Experts note that coherence or incoherence in testimony does not automatically prove that someone is truthful or untruthful; courts must assess evidence carefully. Survivors may have strong emotional reactions, numbness, or confusion when recalling trafficking experiences, and these responses are common after trauma. [eprints.bournemouth.ac](https://eprints.bournemouth.ac.uk/33634/3/SMITH%20trauma,%20memory%20and%20evidence%20with%20comments.pdf)

### C. Asking for pauses, clarification, water, interpreters, or accommodations

- **General courtroom norms**  
  Witness‑support materials emphasize that witnesses may ask for questions to be repeated or explained and may say “I don’t know” instead of guessing. Court guides also note that witnesses can tell the judge if they need a break, feel confused, or need to use the bathroom. [domesticpeace](https://domesticpeace.ca/images/uploads/documents/MicrosoftWord-InformationandTipsforTestifyinginCriminalCourtAugust2009.pdf)

- **How to ask in practice (process only)**  
  Survivors can use simple statements like “I’m confused by that question,” “Can you say it again more slowly?” or “Your Honor, I need a short break,” addressed to the judge or, when appropriate, via the prosecutor. For interpreters or disability accommodations, survivors or their advocates can follow local ADA request procedures as described earlier. [miwd.uscourts](https://www.miwd.uscourts.gov/sites/miwd/files/RHB_Courtroom_Protocol.pdf)

### D. Safe preparation boundaries for advocates

- **What advocates may explain**  
  Trauma‑informed protocols for supporting witnesses encourage advocates to explain court roles, typical steps (such as direct and cross‑examination), and practical details like clothing, transport, and safety plans. They may also teach general coping skills and normalize common emotional reactions. [humanrights.stanford](https://humanrights.stanford.edu/sites/humanrights/files/media/file/2104429-trauma-informed_investigations_field_guide_web_0_0.pdf)

- **What must be left to the survivor’s own words**  
  Ethical and legal guidance warns against shaping, rehearsing, or scripting testimony, including discouraging advocates from telling survivors which facts to share or how to describe events. Advocates should avoid suggesting specific answers, editing survivors’ narratives, or practicing detailed “storylines,” and instead support survivors’ ability to speak in their own words. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/sites/g/files/xyckuh226/files/pubs/OVC_Archives/nvaa99/chap17.htm)

## 5. CROSS‑EXAMINATION: PROCESS, NOT SCRIPTS

### A. What cross‑examination is and why questions may be short or yes/no

- **Definition and purpose**  
  Cross‑examination is questioning by the opposing side’s lawyer after direct examination, designed to test the witness’s observations and statements under the rules of evidence. In criminal trials, cross‑examination is a core part of the adversarial process and is subject to judge oversight. [flnd.uscourts](https://www.flnd.uscourts.gov/role-judge-and-other-courtroom-participants)

- **Question style**  
  Leading questions—questions suggesting an answer—are generally permitted on cross‑examination and often take a “yes” or “no” form. Lawyers may repeat questions, focus on details, or move quickly between topics as part of their strategy, and this can feel uncomfortable. [domesticpeace](https://domesticpeace.ca/images/uploads/documents/MicrosoftWord-InformationandTipsforTestifyinginCriminalCourtAugust2009.pdf)

### B. General rules: answer only what is asked, ask for repetition or clarification, and do not guess

- **Witness guidance from official and reputable materials**  
  Court and witness‑support guides advise witnesses to: listen carefully; answer only the question asked; avoid volunteering extra information unless directed; say “I don’t know” or “I don’t remember” rather than guessing; and ask that confusing questions be repeated or broken into simpler parts. [custodyxchange](https://www.custodyxchange.com/topics/custody/steps/testify-in-family-court.php)

- **Trauma‑informed emphasis**  
  Trauma‑informed resources remind supporters to help witnesses understand these norms without criticizing their natural reactions or pressuring them to respond in a particular way. [courts.michigan](https://www.courts.michigan.gov/4a1c95/siteassets/offices/cwjjs/handouts-trauma-informed-lgal-7.23.24.pdf)

### C. Communicating discomfort or need for a break through court processes

- **Mechanisms**  
  Guides for child and vulnerable witnesses state that if something is bothering a witness or they need a break, they should tell the judge, who can pause proceedings. Similar guidance appears in trauma‑informed court materials, which recommend clear rules about breaks and respect for emotional needs. [courts.michigan](https://www.courts.michigan.gov/4a1c95/siteassets/offices/cwjjs/handouts-trauma-informed-lgal-7.23.24.pdf)

- **Role of prosecutors, victim‑witness staff, and advocates**  
  Survivors can alert these professionals before or during hearings if intense distress or safety concerns arise, so they can ask the court for adjustments within legal limits. [popcenter.asu](https://popcenter.asu.edu/sites/default/files/problems/witness_intimidation/PDFs/USDOJ_OAG_2000.pdf)

### D. Boundaries around sexual‑history evidence (Federal Rule of Evidence 412 and complexity of exceptions)

- **Basic rule**  
  Federal Rule of Evidence 412 generally prohibits admission of evidence offered to prove that an alleged victim engaged in other sexual behavior or has a sexual predisposition in criminal and civil cases involving alleged sexual misconduct. This rule aims to protect victims from invasive sexual‑history inquiries and inappropriate credibility attacks. [casemine](https://www.casemine.com/commentary/us/impact-of-federal-rule-of-evidence-412-and-jury-instructions-on-sex-trafficking-convictions:-analysis-of-rivera-v.-united-states/view)

- **Exceptions and procedures**  
  FRE 412 has specific exceptions—for example, certain evidence of prior sexual behavior may be allowed to show source of semen or injury, or in some civil cases—and requires a written motion and an in camera hearing in many situations. State rape‑shield laws have their own exceptions and procedures, often requiring in‑court hearings before such evidence is used. These rules are complex and must be applied by lawyers and judges on a case‑by‑case basis. [cali](https://www.cali.org/sites/default/files/EvidenceRapeShield_Miller_Dec2014.pdf)

- **Trauma‑informed note**  
  Survivors should be told, in plain language, that courts often limit questions about past sexual history, but that exceptions exist and only a qualified attorney can explain how the rules might apply in their case. [law.cornell](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/fre/rule_412)

### Card 7  
- **Title:** Answering questions safely  
- **Body:**  
  Court materials say you can ask for a question to be repeated or explained if you do not understand it. You can say “I don’t know” or “I don’t remember” instead of guessing. You usually should answer only the question asked and not add extra details unless the judge tells you to. [custodyxchange](https://www.custodyxchange.com/topics/custody/steps/testify-in-family-court.php)

### Card 8  
- **Title:** Trauma and memory  
- **Body:**  
  Research shows trauma can affect memory in different ways. Some people remember events clearly; others have gaps or memories that feel out of order. This is common and does not, by itself, prove whether someone is right or wrong. Emotional reactions like fear, numbness, or confusion can also show up in court. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4183265/)

### Card 9  
- **Title:** Rape‑shield rules and privacy  
- **Body:**  
  Federal Rule of Evidence 412 usually stops lawyers from asking about your past sexual behavior or “sexual predisposition” in sexual‑offense cases, unless special exceptions apply. States have their own versions of this rule. Judges decide when any sexual‑history questions are allowed. [cali](https://www.cali.org/sites/default/files/EvidenceRapeShield_Miller_Dec2014.pdf)
- **Ask your advocate:**  
  Ask a prosecutor or victim‑witness staff how rape‑shield rules work in your case and court. [ovc.ojp](https://ovc.ojp.gov/library/publications/attorney-general-guidelines-victim-and-witness-assistance-2022-edition)

### Card 10  
- **Title:** Your voice at sentencing  
- **Body:**  
  Federal law says crime victims have the right to be “reasonably heard” at sentencing, often through a victim impact statement. You can choose to speak or write about how the crime affected you, within court rules. This is different from your trial testimony. [justia](https://www.justia.com/criminal/docs/frcrimp/rule32/)
- **Ask your advocate:**  
  Ask your advocate how victim impact statements work and what support is available. [ussc](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/primers/2023_Primer_Crime_Victims.pdf)

Requests should be made as early as possible. [probonodeskmanual.loyno](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/court-access-individuals-disabilities/513-possible-accommodations)
